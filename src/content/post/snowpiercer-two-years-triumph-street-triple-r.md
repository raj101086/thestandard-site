I couldn't get a flight home for Diwali. Every option was either sold out or absurd. I sat with it for a while, then opened Google Maps, looked at the route from Pune to Bhilai, and thought: why not.

Bhilai is my hometown. 1,050 kilometres from Pune, give or take. I'd done that ride before on my Interceptor — stopped overnight in Aurangabad because it got dark and I'd run out of road. That was a different machine and a different version of the plan.

I packed a bag, threw it on the bike, and left.

I reached Bhilai the same evening. Parked outside, walked straight to the kitchen. My mother was making sweets for the pooja the next morning. She turned around and saw me standing there with a backpack and a helmet, and for a moment she didn't say anything at all.

That's what this motorcycle is.

Not the 765cc triple. Not the 0-100 time or the cornering clearance or any of the things you'd read in a review. Those things are real, and they matter, but they're not the point. The point is that when I decided on an impulse to ride 1,050 kilometres to surprise my parents on Diwali, the only thing I had to think about was whether I wanted to do it. The bike was never the question.

I named it Snowpiercer after the Bong Joon Ho film — the train carrying the last survivors of the human race, circling a frozen planet, never stopping. It seemed right. This bike doesn't want to stop. It drinks fuel like it means it once you open the throttle, and I've made my peace with that. Two years, 25,000 kilometres, and I'm still learning how to use it properly.

Crystal White. July 2024. A constant smile.

Some machines are just transportation. Others become the reason you go.
