I don't know how to describe what I felt reading the last few pages of this book.

The novel tells you everything upfront. William Stoner goes to university, falls into literature by accident, becomes a teacher, marries the wrong woman, lives a quiet unremarkable life, and dies. That's the whole story, given to you on page one.

And somehow it doesn't matter. You read it anyway, and it gets to you.

I think it hit me because I'm also living a fairly uneventful life. Nothing dramatic, nothing particularly remarkable. The texture of a life where the big moments are quiet and the days mostly just pass. And the book made me sit with that instead of looking away from it. What is the significance of an ordinary life? Stoner doesn't have an answer. Neither does Williams. Neither do I. The book just holds the question in front of you for 300 pages.

It makes you look at your own small decisions differently. A conversation you didn't have. A direction you didn't take. The ripple effect isn't dramatic. It's just your life, gradually becoming what it is.

There's also something grimly appropriate about the book's own story. Published in 1965, barely noticed, sold a few thousand copies. Williams died in 1994. Decades later it gets rediscovered and now everyone calls it a masterpiece. A novel about a man whose life seemed insignificant until someone looked more closely, sharing the same fate.

The only thing that stood between Stoner and a better life was Stoner himself.
