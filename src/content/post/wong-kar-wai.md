My introduction to Wong Kar-wai came through Chungking Express. Then In the Mood for Love. Then 2046 and Fallen Angels.

His films are not for everyone. They're not in English. The stories feel fragmented. Sometimes nothing much happens at all. Long silences, lingering shots, conversations left unfinished, emotions left unspoken. If you want a tight plot, you'll spend most of it wondering what the fuss is about.

And then you rewind a scene for the third time because you can't believe how beautiful it looks.

That's what got me. As someone who shoots photography, I was pulled in by the cinematography before the story. I went down a YouTube rabbit hole trying to figure out what cameras and lenses Christopher Doyle used on Chungking Express. For a few days my Instagram feed was nothing but clips from Wong Kar-wai films, edits set to California Dreamin', and Quizás, Quizás, Quizás.

Chungking Express feels alive. The frantic motion, the neon, the crowded streets of Hong Kong. Wong Kar-wai and Doyle turn a city into something electric. Every frame looks spontaneous, like they caught something that wasn't supposed to be caught.

In the Mood for Love is a different thing entirely. Slow pans, narrow corridors, rain-soaked streets, glances that do more work than any dialogue could. It's a film about two people who fall in love and do nothing about it. That restraint is the whole point, and it's devastating.

I lived in China briefly, which made these films hit differently. In the Mood for Love captures a Hong Kong that's mostly gone now. Watching it feels less like a film and more like looking through someone else's old photographs and recognising the feeling anyway.

Wong Kar-wai's films are about what doesn't happen. The missed chances, the half-finished conversations, the moments that stay with you precisely because nothing was resolved. Start with Chungking Express. See where it takes you.
